Methodological Consequences

(I) The interdisciplinary application of provinces of meaning requires a particularly consistent professional epoch of the social scientist. With a consequent grounding in the "paramount reality" of the lifeworld, motives and premises from other disciplines are bracketed. (No presumption of rule or hegemony.)

(II) The problematic situation in the natural attitude is the starting point for all meaning-making. It is thus also the starting point for the constitution of provinces of meaning as Schlussinstanz.

(III) Thee original relation of mastery is not binding. Due to historical processes of knowledge creation, institutionalization and differentiation based on the division of labour, provinces of meaning may take on new capacities-for and areas-of mastery.

Methodological Consequences

(V) The benchmark and starting point of every interpretative sociology remains the *historical knowledge creation of society in action*, which can and must be interdisciplinary informed. None the less, the description of the social behaviour of lived - and thus lifeworld-embedded - experience offers the essential point of entry.
